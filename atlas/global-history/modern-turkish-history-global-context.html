Theme

14 of 14 eras

Most people learn Turkish history as a national story — sultans, reformers, Atatürk, coups. That framing hides the single most important fact about it: almost nothing that happened in modern Turkey was unique to Turkey. Turkey kept colliding with the same eight pressures that swept every agrarian society on earth between roughly 1750 and today.

European military expansion → industrialization → nationalism → constitutionalism → total war → nation-state construction → Cold War bipolarity → globalization and populism. Each of these was, at root, an idea or a structural force that started somewhere specific — usually in Europe — and then radiated outward. Constitutionalism was invented in England, refined in Philadelphia and Paris, and packaged for export in Brussels. The disciplined standing army was perfected in Sweden and Prussia. The nation-manufacturing school system was pioneered in Third Republic France. Neoliberalism was hatched in a Swiss hotel and first tested in Chile.

So for each era, the useful question is not "What happened in Turkey?" but a three-part one:

  1. Where did this force begin, and why? (usually a European origin)
  2. How did it travel — through which exiles, students, defeats, translations, and treaties?
  3. How did the Ottomans and Turks respond — and how did other non-European societies respond to the same pressure?

A thread worth watching the whole way through: when non-European modernizers went shopping for a model, most of them did not pick the liberal British or French version. They picked the Prussian/German version — constitutional in form, but with a strong monarch, a powerful bureaucracy, and a semi-autonomous army. That one choice — modernize the state without surrendering the state — echoes from Abdülhamid all the way to the present.

One correction, verified

The third major republican coup happened on September 12, 1980, not 1989. The year 1989 belongs to a different story — the fall of communism across Eastern Europe — that nonetheless helped shape Turkey's own 1980s.

Part I
The Imperial Century
Era 01 c. 1770 – 1839

The First Military-Administrative Reform Era

Global question: How can an old agrarian empire survive against scientifically organized, professionally armed European states?

Where it began · Europe's Military Revolution

Long before Istanbul felt the pressure, Europe had quietly rebuilt the art of war. Historians call it the Military Revolution (a thesis from Michael Roberts in 1955, expanded by Geoffrey Parker). Between roughly 1560 and 1720, European armies changed shape: drill and discipline (Maurice of Nassau's Dutch reforms broke soldiers into endlessly-drilled units firing in rotation); the star fort (trace italienne), which made sieges ruinously expensive; the standing army (Gustavus Adolphus of Sweden, then Louis XIV's France under Louvois); and above all the fiscal-military state — John Brewer's phrase for the way a modern army forces the creation of modern taxation, public debt, and bureaucracy. Prussia under Frederick the Great was the extreme case: a poor state that became an army with a country attached.

The buried lesson — the one every non-European reformer would learn the hard way — is that you cannot import the army without importing the state behind it. Muskets are cheap; the tax system, officer schools, and logistics that make them lethal are not.

How it traveled

Through defeat, and through hired experts. The Ottomans learned the new math of war on the battlefield against Russia — a state that had itself imported the European model a lifetime earlier. They also hired European advisors directly: the French officer Baron de Tott modernized Ottoman artillery and founded a school of military engineering in the 1770s. Permanent Ottoman embassies in European capitals, established under Selim III, turned diplomats into intelligence-gatherers on European institutions.

The Ottoman response

The empire was not collapsing because its rulers "became incompetent." The environment had changed, and institutions that were brilliant in 1550 — the Janissary corps, tax farming, semi-autonomous governors — had become liabilities for industrializing warfare.

  • Selim III's Nizam-ı Cedid ("New Order," 1790s): a European-style army raised alongside the old one, new revenue to fund it, engineering schools, permanent embassies. It failed politically because it created losers — Janissaries, provincial notables, and religious figures who deposed Selim in 1807.
  • Mahmud II (r. 1808–1839): struck harder. In 1826, in the Vaka-i Hayriye ("Auspicious Incident"), he destroyed the Janissary corps outright — by cannon fire in their barracks — then built a new army, broke the provincial notables, expanded the bureaucracy, and introduced censuses, quarantine, a postal service, and the fez. Not yet Westernization: state survival through centralization.

How other societies responded to the same pressure

  • Russia (Peter the Great, c. 1700): got there first — a Western army and navy, compulsory noble service, a new capital at St. Petersburg. Militarized, state-centered, not remotely liberal.
  • Egypt (Muhammad Ali, r. 1805–1848): in some ways more radical than Istanbul — conscription, military schools, state industry, student missions to Europe — but hung on one strongman and coercive extraction.
  • Qing China: responded late; only after the Opium Wars did the Self-Strengthening Movement buy Western weapons while leaving the Confucian order untouched.
  • Tokugawa Japan: watched, and after Commodore Perry's warships forced its ports open (1853–54), concluded that buying weapons was not enough — the institutions had to change. That conclusion would produce the Meiji Restoration.

The shared dilemma, stated plainly: can we borrow Western military power without letting Western ideas transform our politics? Almost everywhere, the honest answer was no — because a modern army drags modern taxation, schooling, and law in behind it.

Era 02 1839 – 1876

Tanzimat & the Global Age of State Reconstruction

Global question: How can a multiethnic empire become a modern, centralized state without disintegrating?

Where it began · the Napoleonic administrative state

If Era 1 was about the army, Era 2 was about the bureaucracy — and its template was Napoleonic France. The French Revolution and Napoleon fused several world-changing ideas into a working machine: the citizen replaces the subject (people as individuals equal before one state law, not members of a religious or corporate order); the Napoleonic Code (1804), a single uniform civil law; and rational-legal bureaucracy — prefects, meritocratic exams, salaried officials, standardized paperwork. Max Weber would later name this "rational-legal authority": power that flows from written rules and offices, not from birth or sacred status. Prussia added its famously professional civil service. The European message: a modern state runs on documents, exams, and salaries — not on households and hereditary status.

How it traveled

Through a specific institution and a specific generation. The Ottoman Translation Office (Tercüme Odası), created after Greek interpreters became unreliable during the Greek revolt, became an unintended school for reformers — young men who learned French, read European law, and rose to run the empire. Mustafa Reşid Pasha, who drafted the founding Tanzimat edict, had served as ambassador in Paris and London and had seen the machine up close. His protégés Âli Pasha and Fuad Pasha ran the reform era.

The Ottoman response · the Tanzimat

The Tanzimat ("Reordering") ran from the Gülhane Edict of 1839 to the first constitution in 1876. It faced four interlocking dangers — European military superiority, provincial fragmentation, rising nationalism (Greek independence in 1830 proved a Christian population could mobilize and win European backing), and European "intervention" on behalf of Ottoman Christians. Its response had four parts:

  • Recast subjects as citizens (Ottomanism): the 1839 and 1856 edicts promised security of life, honor, and property, regular taxation, and orderly conscription for all subjects regardless of religion.
  • Build a modern bureaucracy: ministries, salaried officials, the 1864 Provincial Law, censuses, state schools.
  • Create legal duality: new commercial and criminal courts beside the sharia courts — a lattice of overlapping jurisdictions, not instant secularism.
  • Expand modern schools that trained officers, doctors, engineers — and, unintentionally, the constitutional intellectuals of the next era.

How other societies responded · the great Meiji comparison

The obvious parallel is Meiji Japan, but the two were not twins. Japan reformed by overthrowing the Tokugawa shogunate in the emperor's name; the Ottomans reformed within the existing dynasty. Japan was a compact island facing colonization; the Ottomans were a sprawling multiethnic empire facing breakup and Great-Power intervention. Japan looked more "successful" — but for structural reasons, not moral ones. It had geographic insulation, few foreign-protected minorities, a unified post-Restoration elite, and the ability to dissolve hereditary domains cleanly. The Ottomans had to modernize while fighting wars, servicing crushing debt (they went bankrupt in 1875), and managing a dozen nationalisms. Alexander II's Russia ran parallel "Great Reforms" (emancipating the serfs in 1861); Egypt and Qajar Persia tried lighter versions and slid toward European financial control.

"Japan modernized properly, the Ottomans failed" is lazy history. Both were solving the same problem — Japan simply started from an easier board.

Era 03 1860s – 1908

Constitutionalism & the Young Ottomans

Global question: Can constitutional government save the empire by limiting arbitrary rule and manufacturing loyalty?

Where it began · the long European road to the written constitution

This is where the "European origin, global diffusion" pattern is clearest, so it's worth tracing the whole lineage:

  • England (the deep root): Magna Carta (1215) planted the idea that even a king is under law; the Glorious Revolution (1688) and Bill of Rights (1689) established parliamentary supremacy — a constitution never written down.
  • The United States (1787): the first written national constitution — one document creating and limiting a government, with separated powers and rights.
  • France (1789–91): the Declaration of the Rights of Man and the Constitution of 1791 turned constitutionalism into a universal, exportable creed: sovereignty belongs to the nation, not the crown.
  • 1848 — the "Springtime of Peoples": revolutions swept Europe demanding constitutions and parliaments. Most were crushed; the demand became permanent.
  • The Belgian Constitution of 1831 — the export model. Belgium's liberal charter (bicameral parliament, ministerial responsibility, guaranteed rights, a constrained monarch) became the single most copied constitutional template of the 19th century — reached for from Istanbul to Tokyo to Tehran, often literally, because it was written in French.

Note the fork in the road: a liberal model (Britain, Belgium, France — parliament on top) and a monarchical-statist model (Prussia after 1850 — constitution in form, strong king and army in fact). Non-European modernizers overwhelmingly chose the Prussian fork. It let them look modern while keeping the center strong.

How it traveled · the Young Ottomans in exile

Tanzimat schools had produced a class the reformers never intended: journalists and jurists fluent in European political thought who now asked an awkward question — if bureaucrats are modernizing the country in the people's name, who holds the bureaucrats accountable? The Young Ottomans — Namık Kemal, Ziya Pasha, Ali Suavi — fled to Paris and London in the 1860s, published opposition papers (notably Hürriyet, "Liberty"), and read Montesquieu and Rousseau. Their real innovation was translation in the deep sense: they argued constitutional government was not foreign but an Islamic obligation, grounded in meşveret (consultation) and şûra (consultative decision-making). Constitutionalism arrived in Istanbul wearing Islamic dress.

The Ottoman response

  • The Constitution of 1876 (Kanun-ı Esasi): drafted by a commission under Midhat Pasha (with Namık Kemal and Ziya Pasha), modeled — this is now well documented — on the Belgian Constitution of 1831 (bicameral parliament, ministerial responsibility) and the Prussian Constitution of 1850 (strong monarch). It created a written constitution, an elected lower chamber, and formal Ottoman citizenship.
  • The reversal: after catastrophic defeat in the Russo-Turkish War, Sultan Abdülhamid II suspended parliament in February 1878 and exiled Midhat Pasha.
  • Abdülhamid's answer — authoritarian modernization: he chose the Prussian fork to the extreme — centralized palace rule, a huge expansion of modern schools, telegraphs, railways (the Hejaz Railway), intelligence networks, and pan-Islamic legitimacy — plus censorship and surveillance. Modern administration without open politics.

How other societies responded · the same wave, the same fork

  • Meiji Japan (1889): the cleanest case of the Prussian choice. Itō Hirobumi toured Europe in 1882–83, studied under Rudolf von Gneist in Berlin and Lorenz von Stein in Vienna, and rejected the British and American models as "too liberal." He built a constitution (promulgated 1889, Diet convened 1890) granting a parliament but reserving sovereignty and command of the army to a sacred, "inviolable" emperor. Japan wanted to modernize without democratizing — and copied Prussia to do it.
  • Russia (1905–06): defeat by Japan plus revolution forced the October Manifesto and a parliament (the Duma) — which the tsar then repeatedly gutted.
  • Iran (1906): the Constitutional Revolution won a parliament (Majlis) and a constitution, before Russian and British intervention strangled it.
  • China: late-Qing "New Policies" promised constitutional government too slowly to prevent the 1911 revolution.

Constitutionalism outside Europe was not mimicry. It grew wherever modernizing states created taxpayers who wanted accountability, officers who wanted competence, and merchants who wanted predictable law.

Era 04 1908 – 1914

The Young Turk Revolution & the Global Revolutionary Wave

Global question: Can a constitutional revolution prevent imperial collapse?

Where it began · revolution meets scientific politics

Two European currents converged. One was the revolutionary constitutional tradition running from 1789 through 1848. The other was positivism — Auguste Comte's faith that society could be governed scientifically by an enlightened technical elite, under the banner "Order and Progress." The Young Turks' Paris theorist Ahmed Rıza was a committed Comtean; the movement's French name, the Committee of Union and Progress, borrowed Comte's slogan. This matters: the CUP wanted not just liberty but scientific, unifying, elite-led government — a tension that would push it toward authoritarianism.

How it traveled & the Ottoman response

Through exiled intellectuals in Paris and Geneva, and — decisively — through junior officers of the Third Army in Macedonia, the empire's most exposed frontier. They organized in secret and watched the Great Powers circle the Balkans. In July 1908 they forced Abdülhamid II to restore the constitution and reopen parliament. The opening weeks brought euphoria — Muslims, Christians, Jews, Turks, Arabs, Armenians, and Greeks celebrating "constitutional fraternity" in the streets.

Then reality arrived in a rush: Austria-Hungary annexed Bosnia (1908), Bulgaria declared independence, Italy invaded Libya (1911), and the Balkan Wars (1912–13) stripped away almost all of Ottoman Europe. Under siege, the CUP grew steadily more centralized, nationalist, and authoritarian — ending in a one-party wartime dictatorship.

How other societies responded · a genuine global pulse

1908 was one beat in a remarkable rhythm of constitutional revolutions: Russia (1905) — a parliament wrung from an autocrat who clawed power back; Iran (1906) — a constitution won, then undermined by foreign intervention; China (1911) — the Qing dynasty fell, but the republic inherited warlordism; Portugal (1910) — the monarchy overthrown, the First Republic proclaimed; Mexico (1910) — revolution against Díaz, producing the landmark 1917 constitution.

The pattern that connects them: constitutional revolutions repeatedly broke the old center before a stable new order had formed. The Young Turks restored a constitution and lost an empire within a decade.

Era 05 1912 – 1923

Total War & the Collapse of Empires

Global question: When an empire dies, what replaces it — dynasty, religion, ethnicity, or territorial citizenship?

Where it began · the European invention of total war

Modern Turkey was not born at a peace conference. It was born inside the most destructive idea Europe ever exported: total war — war that mobilizes the entire population, economy, and moral energy of a nation. The French Revolution (1793) invented the levée en masse, conscripting a whole nation and turning war from the king's business into the nation's. The American Civil War (1861–65) was arguably the first industrial total war — railroads, telegraphs, mass-produced weapons, and the deliberate targeting of an enemy society's economy. World War I fused the two: mass conscript armies plus full industrial mobilization, at a casualty scale no one had imagined.

What it did to the Ottoman world

The Ottomans entered this machine already bleeding. The Balkan Wars (1912–13) were a psychological earthquake: enormous territorial loss, waves of Muslim refugees fleeing the Balkans and Caucasus, and a hardening of Turkish-Muslim nationalism. The empire then entered World War I alongside Germany — partly out of fear of isolation and partition — fighting at Gallipoli, in the Caucasus, in Mesopotamia, and in Palestine.

The human cost was catastrophic and must be named plainly. The war brought mass death to Ottoman Muslims and to Christian populations — above all the Armenian Genocide of 1915–16, the systematic destruction of the empire's Armenian population, alongside mass killings of Assyrians and Anatolian Greeks. An honest global-context account holds two truths at once: the geopolitical collapse of an empire, and the immense, deliberate suffering inflicted on civilians within it.

How other societies responded · four empires fall at once

Between 1917 and 1923, four dynastic empires vanished together: Romanov Russia → revolution, civil war, a new Soviet federation; Habsburg Austria-Hungary → shattered into Austria, Hungary, Czechoslovakia, Yugoslavia, and enlarged Romania and Poland; Hohenzollern Germany → a republic that kept its army and bureaucracy, with a humiliation that later fed Nazism; and the Ottoman Empire → occupation and the punitive Treaty of Sèvres (1920), which would have dismembered Anatolia itself.

What made Turkey distinctive

The Turkish nationalist movement under Mustafa Kemal did what Vienna, Berlin, and Petrograd could not: it revised the postwar settlement by force. It built an alternative parliament in Ankara (1920), fought the occupying forces to a standstill, rejected Sèvres, abolished the sultanate (1922), and won recognition of a sovereign Turkey at the Treaty of Lausanne (1923) — the only Central Powers state to tear up its dictated peace and negotiate a better one. But the republic that emerged was a different creature: smaller, far more religiously homogeneous (partly through the Greek-Turkish population exchange), more centralized, and explicitly national rather than imperial and plural.

Turkey was the one defeated empire that tore up its dictated peace and negotiated a better one — and in doing so, traded a plural empire for a national state.

Part II
The Republic & the Cold War
Era 06 1923 – 1945

Kemalist Nation-State Construction

Global question: How do you turn the wreckage of an empire into a modern nation-state?

Where it began · France teaches the world to manufacture a nation

Here the European model is precise and often overlooked. The paradigm is the French Third Republic, and the classic account is Eugen Weber's Peasants into Frenchmen (1976). Weber showed that as late as 1870, millions of "French" people did not speak French or feel loyalty to Paris. Over the next decades the Republic manufactured Frenchmen with four tools: universal, compulsory, secular primary schooling (the Jules Ferry laws, 1880s), teaching one national language and history; mass conscription that mixed regional boys into a national army; railways and roads that broke village isolation; and a war on regional languages in favor of standard French. Add French laïcité — the 1905 separation of church and state — and you have the Kemalist toolkit almost point for point. The backdrop was German Romantic nationalism (Herder, Fichte: a nation is its language) fused with French positivist state-building.

How it traveled

Through the late-Ottoman schools that trained the republican elite in exactly this French positivist, secular-nationalist tradition — and through one theorist above all: Ziya Gökalp, who adapted Émile Durkheim to argue that Turks needed a manufactured, unifying national culture. Kemal's circle had lived the empire's failure and concluded that its weakness came from divided loyalties, religious fragmentation, low literacy, and too little national consciousness.

The Turkish response · state-manufactured nationhood

  • Political: abolished the sultanate (1922) and the caliphate (1924) — ending the office that symbolically led Sunni Islam — then consolidated one-party rule under the Republican People's Party (CHP).
  • Legal: adopted the Swiss Civil Code (1926) wholesale, with civil marriage, secular courts, and redefined citizenship.
  • Cultural: the Latin alphabet reform (1928), language purification, a new national historiography, dress reform, and laïcité written into the state's identity.
  • Economic: after a liberal start, a turn to étatism in the 1930s — state banks, factories, railways, Five-Year Plans, protected industry.

How other societies responded · the interwar nation-building wave

  • Reza Shah's Iran (r. 1925–41): the closest parallel — centralization, a national army, secular schools, dress reform, control of the clergy. But Reza Shah kept the monarchy; Kemal abolished both dynasty and caliphate.
  • The Soviet Union: shared the tools — mass literacy, planning, a controlling party, a rewritten history — but pursued communist class transformation and abolished private property, which Turkey never did.
  • Post-revolutionary Mexico: limited the Church, built mass public education, forged a national identity (partly through the muralists), and ruled through a single dominant party (the PRI) — a strikingly close structural cousin.
  • Fascist Italy and Nazi Germany: Turkey shared some surface features — one-party rule, a leader cult, mass nationalism — but lacked the expansionist, racial-totalitarian, mass-party system of fascism.

The right category is authoritarian developmental nation-building, not fascism — the same family as Iran and Mexico, racing to build a nation before the world's next crisis arrived.

Era 07 1945 – 1960

Democratization & Entry into the American-Led Order

Global question: How does Turkey secure itself against the Soviet Union — and buy its way into the Western club?

Where it began · the American liberal international order

This force originated across the Atlantic — though it reshaped Europe first. Out of World War II the United States built a new order: Bretton Woods (1944), the dollar-anchored monetary system with the IMF and World Bank; containment (George Kennan's 1946–47 doctrine that the USSR must be resisted at every point of pressure); and — the detail most people miss — the Truman Doctrine (1947), which launched the entire Cold War aid system and was written for Greece and Turkey. Truman's March 1947 speech asking Congress to fund those two frontier states was the founding act of containment. The Marshall Plan (1948) and NATO (1949) then rebuilt and re-armed Western Europe under American leadership.

How it traveled & Turkey's response

Turkey sat on the fault line — bordering the USSR, controlling the Straits, anchoring NATO's southeastern flank. Stalin's postwar demands on the Straits and Turkey's eastern border pushed Ankara firmly westward, while one-party rule was losing legitimacy among landowners, merchants, and religious conservatives. Turkey's response married the two pressures: genuine multiparty elections — the opposition Democrat Party won in 1950 (İnönü peacefully handed over power); a brigade to the Korean War (1950), Turkey's blood-price entry ticket; NATO membership (1952); and agricultural mechanization (tractors via Marshall aid) plus road-building that pulled villages into the market.

How other societies responded

  • Greece: the other Truman Doctrine frontier — but democratization was wrecked by civil war and later the Colonels' coup.
  • Italy: joined NATO while a huge Communist Party stayed legal; American aid and Christian Democratic dominance kept it Western.
  • Japan and West Germany: American-supervised democratization and economic revival, becoming anchor allies.
  • South Korea: joined the anti-communist order but stayed authoritarian for decades.

Western alignment did not automatically produce liberal democracy. The United States repeatedly propped up authoritarian allies when they were strategically reliable — a tension that set the stage for the coups to come.

Era 08 1960 – 1971

Military Guardianship & the 1960 Coup

Global question: Who guards the constitutional order when elected governments turn majoritarian or unstable?

Where it began · the theory of the "guardian" army

The modern idea of the military as guardian has European roots and a Cold War theory. The Prussian army had long styled itself a "state within the state," loyal to the nation above any government. In the 1950s–60s two political scientists gave the phenomenon its language: Samuel Huntington (The Soldier and the State, 1957) and Samuel Finer (The Man on Horseback, 1962), who analyzed why modernizing militaries so often intervene against "corrupt" civilian politics. There is also an older Latin American lineage — Brazil's constitutional Poder Moderador ("moderating power") and the caudillo tradition of the strongman-savior. In Turkey the army had a specific founding mission: it was the guardian of Atatürk's secular republic. That self-image is the key to every Turkish coup.

The Turkish response · the 1960 coup

By the late 1950s the Democrat Party government faced inflation, a hostile press, pressure on universities, and open conflict with the CHP and the officer corps. On May 27, 1960, junior and mid-ranking officers seized power. Prime Minister Adnan Menderes and two ministers — Foreign Minister Fatin Rüştü Zorlu and Finance Minister Hasan Polatkan — were tried and executed in 1961, a lasting national trauma.

Here is the paradox: it was not a simple anti-communist coup, and its product was more liberal, not less. The 1961 Constitution created a Constitutional Court, a bicameral legislature, university autonomy, stronger courts, and expanded social and labor rights — arguably the most liberal charter in Turkish history. Yet the same coup normalized the military's belief that it had a standing right to supervise civilian politics. Turkey got freedom and tutelage in the same package.

How other societies responded

  • Pakistan (1958): Ayub Khan justified intervention by corruption and instability — but built a longer-lasting direct military regime.
  • South Korea (1961): Park Chung-hee's coup produced an authoritarian developmental state — export-led growth, industrial planning, iron discipline.
  • Egypt (1952): the Free Officers abolished the monarchy for republicanism, land reform, and Arab nationalism under Nasser.
  • Latin America: militaries across the continent cast themselves as technical and moral guardians rescuing the nation from populism, communism, and disorder.

The "guardian coup" was a global genre. Turkey's twist was a military that intervened, redesigned the rules, and then withdrew — leaving behind a constitution and a permanent claim to oversight rather than a permanent junta.

Era 09 1968 – 1973

Global 1968 & the 1971 Memorandum

Global question: Can parliamentary institutions absorb rapid social change and revolutionary polarization?

Where it began · the global 1968

For once the force was almost simultaneous everywhere, with no single origin — but its intellectual center of gravity was Europe and the New Left. May 1968 in Paris (students and ten million striking workers nearly toppling de Gaulle) became the emblem; the Prague Spring offered a socialist-reform variant; the Frankfurt School (Herbert Marcuse above all) supplied the theory of a rebellion against consumer conformity and imperial war. The shared themes — anti-authoritarianism, anti-imperialism, Marxism, student power — surfaced in Berkeley, Berlin, Rome, Tokyo, Mexico City, and Istanbul at nearly the same moment.

The Turkish response

Turkey experienced the whole syndrome at once: rapid rural-to-urban migration, exploding universities, labor organizing, socialist and Islamist and ultranationalist movements, anti-American demonstrations (the U.S. Sixth Fleet was a recurring flashpoint), and, ominously, the first armed political groups. In March 1971 the military issued a memorandum — not tanks in the street, but a written ultimatum that forced the government to resign and installed a repressive, technocratic administration.

The contrast with 1960 is the point. The 1960 intervention had opened the system (the liberal 1961 constitution). The 1971 memorandum sought to close it — restoring hierarchy, executive authority, and control over radical movements, and clawing back several of the 1961 freedoms.

How other societies responded · same pressure, different outcomes

  • France (1968): absorbed mass revolt without military rule — its civilian institutions held and de Gaulle retained authority.
  • Mexico (1968): the ruling party-state answered students with the Tlatelolco massacre.
  • Greece (1967): the Colonels imposed outright military dictatorship.
  • Brazil (post-1964): the military regime deepened repression as guerrilla movements grew.

Identical social pressures produced wildly different results depending on institutional capacity. Where civilian institutions were strong, democracy bent and held; where brittle, the soldiers stepped in.

Era 10 1973 – 1983

The 1970s Crisis & the September 12, 1980 Coup

Global question: How does a state restore order amid political violence, inflation, energy shocks, and institutional paralysis?

Where it began · the 1970s crisis & the "national security state"

Two forces converged. First, the global economic rupture of the 1970s: the oil shocks of 1973 and 1979 and the resulting stagflation shattered the postwar Keynesian consensus everywhere. Second, a distinct genre of military regime spread across the developing world — what the political scientist Guillermo O'Donnell called the "bureaucratic-authoritarian state": technocrats and generals seizing power to crush the left, discipline labor, and impose economic restructuring, all justified by Cold War anti-communism. Latin America's Southern Cone was the laboratory.

The Turkish response · the 1980 coup

Turkey in the late 1970s was near collapse: left-right political violence killed thousands a year (revolutionary leftists, ultranationalist "Grey Wolves," Kurdish and sectarian actors); a paralyzed parliament could not even elect a president through months of ballots; and the economy was choking on inflation, shortages, and debt. On September 12, 1980, the military under General Kenan Evren seized power — the third major intervention after 1960 and 1971. The junta dissolved parliament, banned all parties, detained hundreds of thousands, used torture and executions, crushed the unions and the left, redesigned the universities, and wrote the deliberately restrictive 1982 Constitution before handing back a tightly-supervised civilian politics in 1983.

On the "NATO ordered it" question

The coup cannot be reduced to a NATO operation; it grew from real domestic violence, state failure, and economic crisis. But it happened inside an international anti-communist security order that treated Turkey as a vital frontier, and Turkey did host NATO-linked "stay-behind" (Gladio-type) networks built for resistance to a Soviet invasion, whose exact relationship to domestic violence remains debated. The honest formulation: NATO context, American strategic preferences, and anti-communism all mattered — but no one at NATO headquarters simply issued the order.

How other societies responded · the global wave of anti-left coups

  • Chile (1973): Pinochet overthrew Allende, unleashed violent repression, and imposed radical market reform.
  • Argentina (1976): the junta's "Dirty War" produced tens of thousands of disappearances.
  • Brazil (1964), Uruguay (1973): technocratic, anti-communist, repressive.
  • Indonesia (1965–66): the annihilation of the Communist Party through mass killing, birthing Suharto's regime.
  • South Korea: authoritarian rule + anti-communism + state-led growth, then eventual controlled democratization — the closest structural match to Turkey's trajectory.

Turkey's military typically withdrew from day-to-day rule relatively fast but left behind constitutions, party bans, and a shrunken political field. The goal was never a permanent junta — it was a military-supervised civilian order.

Part III
Globalization & Its Discontents
Era 11 1980 – 2001

Özal, Neoliberalism & Global Economic Opening

Global question: How does a protected national economy learn to compete in global markets?

Where it began · from a Swiss hotel to Chile to Downing Street

Neoliberalism has an unusually traceable birth. In 1947, a group of economists led by Friedrich Hayek — with Milton Friedman, Ludwig von Mises, and others — founded the Mont Pelerin Society in Switzerland to defend free markets against both socialism and Keynesian planning. For decades it was fringe. Then it found power: Chile (from 1975) became the first laboratory — the "Chicago Boys," Chilean economists trained under Friedman, restructured the economy under Pinochet; Britain (1979) and the United States (1981) mainstreamed it under Thatcher and Reagan — deregulation, privatization, tax cuts, weakened unions; and the "Washington Consensus" (coined by John Williamson in 1989) let the IMF and World Bank export the package worldwide as the price of loans.

How it traveled & Turkey's response

Turkey's old model — import-substitution industrialization (tariffs, state enterprises, currency controls) — had built domestic industry but hit a wall of foreign-exchange shortages and debt by 1979. The transmission mechanism was the classic one: an IMF-backed rescue. The January 24, 1980 decisions, drafted by technocrat Turgut Özal, devalued the currency, slashed controls, and pivoted to export-led growth. Note the timing: these reforms came months before the September coup, which then enforced them by crushing the labor resistance that might have blocked them. As prime minister from 1983 and later president, Özal drove trade opening, financial liberalization, privatization, and integration into global capital.

How other societies responded

  • Britain / United States: the ideological headquarters.
  • Chile: the harshest, earliest laboratory.
  • Deng Xiaoping's China: not a copy — markets, foreign investment, and export zones grafted onto continuing Communist Party control and state-directed strategy.
  • India (1991): a balance-of-payments crisis forced deregulation, lower tariffs, and opening.
  • Latin America broadly: structural adjustment under debt pressure.

Turkey's results were double-edged: real export growth and integration — alongside inequality, volatile capital, chronic inflation, and a string of crises that culminated in the 2001 crash.

Era 12 1983 – 2002

Political Islam, Identity & the 1997 Intervention

Global question: Can religiously rooted movements enter secular democratic systems without triggering state backlash?

Where it began · the return of religion & the Christian Democratic model

This era's European reference points are subtler, because the force was global — but two illuminate it. First, the collapse of the secularization thesis: sociologists had predicted modernity would privatize religion, and by the 1980s that was visibly failing worldwide (the sociologist José Casanova analyzed this "deprivatization" in Public Religions in the Modern World, 1994). Second — the useful comparison — postwar Europe had already produced a successful model of religiously-rooted democratic politics: Christian Democracy. Germany's CDU and Italy's Democrazia Cristiana combined religious identity, democratic participation, and market economics without theocracy — precisely the template Turkey's reformist Islamists would later invoke as "conservative democrats." The global accelerant was the 1979 Iranian Revolution and the broader Islamic revival.

The Turkish response

Turkey was changing underneath its secular state: villagers poured into cities, a pious provincial business class (the "Anatolian tigers") rose, and religious networks provided welfare and solidarity while the old center-right decayed. Necmettin Erbakan's National Outlook (Milli Görüş) movement rode this wave; its Welfare Party (Refah) won not only on religious identity but on local services, social assistance, organizational discipline, and anti-corruption appeal. In 1996 Erbakan became Turkey's first Islamist prime minister. The establishment struck back: on February 28, 1997, the military-dominated National Security Council issued demands that forced Erbakan's resignation — a "postmodern coup" conducted through pressure, media campaigns, and judicial action rather than tanks.

How other societies responded

  • Algeria (1991–92): when the Islamist FIS was poised to win elections, the army cancelled the vote — triggering a civil war that killed perhaps 200,000. This was the catastrophe Turkey did not have.
  • Egypt: the Muslim Brotherhood participated socially and politically but faced repeated repression.
  • Tunisia: Ennahda was crushed under Ben Ali, then re-entered politics after 2011.
  • India: Hindu nationalism grew as a parallel religious-majoritarian movement — through elections.

Turkey avoided Algeria's abyss. A younger generation broke from Erbakan and founded the AK Party — so the February 28 crackdown produced the very party that would dominate the next quarter-century.

Era 13 2002 – 2011

The AK Party & the Democratic-Developmental Moment

Global question: Can markets, integration, and civilian democracy be combined with conservative social identity?

Where it began · the EU's "transformative power"

The defining force here was European, and it had a name in the literature: the EU's "transformative power" or external anchoring. The mechanism was enlargement conditionality — the Copenhagen criteria of 1993, which set democratic and market benchmarks candidates had to meet to join. For post-communist Central and Eastern Europe, the promise of membership became the single most powerful engine of reform in modern history — Poland, Hungary, the Czech Republic and others locked in democracy, markets, and civilian control of the military to "return to Europe" (they joined in 2004). The theory: an external reward can consolidate reforms that domestic politics alone would not sustain.

The Turkish response

The 2001 financial crisis discredited the entire political establishment and cleared the field. The AK Party — benefiting from conservative networks, municipal governing experience, business support, and public hunger for stability — won power in 2002 and used EU accession as its anchor. In its early years the government implemented the fiscal and banking reforms begun under technocrat Kemal Derviş; passed EU harmonization packages expanding rights and the rule of law; rolled back formal military tutelage (curbing the National Security Council); delivered growth, foreign investment, and infrastructure; and opened cautious discussion of the Kurdish question. The accession process supplied external standards, economic credibility, protection against another coup, and a common platform uniting liberals, conservatives, minorities, and business.

How other societies responded

  • Central and Eastern Europe: the paradigm cases of accession-driven consolidation.
  • Brazil under Lula (from 2003): growth plus social inclusion within democratic institutions.
  • Emerging-market democracies broadly: the optimistic early-2000s belief that integration and reform led naturally toward convergence.

External anchoring works only as long as the reward stays credible. When the EU door visibly began to close after 2005–07, the anchor's disciplining power faded — setting up the turn that followed.

Era 14 c. 2011 – present

Authoritarian Centralization & the Global Populist Wave

Global question: What happens when an elected leader claims to embody the authentic nation more fully than institutions do?

Where it began · the theory and the wave of populism

Populism has deep roots — the 1890s U.S. People's Party, the Russian narodniki — but its modern analysis is recent and sharp. Political scientists Cas Mudde (populism as a "thin-centered ideology" dividing society into "the pure people" versus "the corrupt elite") and Jan-Werner Müller (What Is Populism?, 2016, on populism's core anti-pluralism — the claim that the leader alone represents the real people) gave the phenomenon its modern vocabulary. The contemporary wave crested in the 2010s: Hungary's Viktor Orbán — who in 2014 openly embraced "illiberal democracy" — became the paradigm (win elections, then rewrite constitutional rules and capture courts and media), with parallels in Chávez's Venezuela, Poland's PiS, Modi's India, Putin's Russia, and populists across the Americas and Europe. The common script: use majoritarian legitimacy to weaken the institutional constraints that populists recast as obstacles to "the popular will."

The Turkish response

Turkey's turn toward centralization was not one event but an accumulation: the fading of EU credibility; conflict over control of the judiciary and bureaucracy; the Gezi Park protests (2013); the collapse of the AK Party's former alliance with the Gülen movement; the spillover of the Syrian war and refugee crisis; renewed conflict with the Kurdish PKK; a wave of terrorism; and — the hinge event — the failed coup attempt of July 15, 2016, followed by a state of emergency and mass purges. The 2017 constitutional referendum replaced the parliamentary system with a strong executive presidency, concentrating power in the president's office.

How other societies responded

  • Hungary: the closest template — electoral victories converted into constitutional and media capture.
  • India: electoral legitimacy fused with religious-national identity and digital mobilization, in a very different federal setting.
  • Russia: further down the same road but more dependent on security services, controlled elections, and energy revenue — Turkey retains more genuine electoral competition, so full equivalence misleads.
  • Latin American populism (left and right): direct leader-to-voter communication, plebiscitary politics, and narratives of national restoration.

Turkey's trajectory is not exceptional — it is one national instance of a worldwide pattern in which elected majorities erode the checks that make elections meaningful — yet competitive elections remain real enough to matter, as opposition municipal victories after 2019 showed.

The synthesis

Five principles for reading Turkish history globally

1. Turkey is rarely unique — its combination is

Military reform, constitutionalism, nationalism, authoritarian modernization, coups, neoliberalism, and populism all appeared across dozens of societies. What is distinctive about Turkey is not any single element but the particular sequence and combination in which it experienced them — and the unusual persistence of a strong-state instinct through every phase.

2. The goal was almost never "become European" — it was "stay sovereign"

Ottoman and Turkish rulers were rarely asking "How do we become culturally European?" They were asking "How do we get the institutions we need to survive as an independent state?" The driving logic, from Selim III to today, is defensive modernization — and that is exactly why so many reformers chose the Prussian/statist model over the liberal British or French one. They wanted the strength of modernity without surrendering control of the state.

3. Every reform breeds the actors of the next crisis

This is the deepest pattern in the whole story:

  • Military schools produced the political officers who staged coups.
  • Tanzimat schools produced the constitutional intellectuals who demanded parliaments.
  • Markets produced the pious Anatolian business class that funded political Islam.
  • Urbanization produced both the leftist and the Islamist movements of the 1970s–90s.
  • Free elections produced the mass parties — and eventually the personalized populism — that reshaped the state.

Reform never merely solves a problem. It manufactures the social forces behind the next conflict.

4. The coups were neither purely domestic nor foreign-ordered

It is inadequate to say "the coups were entirely home-grown" or "NATO/Washington carried them out." The stronger account holds several factors in tension at once: domestic polarization, weak civilian institutions, a guardian-army ideology, class conflict, economic crisis, Cold War anti-communism, alliance structures, and the international system's tolerance of authoritarian allies. The truth lives in the interaction, not in either monocause.

5. Modern Turkish history is one long argument between two state-building instincts

One instinct says: a strong state must discipline society to protect independence and order. The other says: a legitimate state must let society represent itself through pluralistic institutions. From Tanzimat to today, Turkish politics keeps re-fighting this same battle — which yields the deepest recurring question of the whole tradition:

How can a strategically exposed, socially diverse, rapidly changing country build a state strong enough to survive — but limited enough not to dominate its own society?

That question was open in 1839. It is still open now.
